Heating the World, 0.08°C at a time!?

According to NASA, The world is getting warmer. Global temperature records start around 1880 because observations did not sufficiently cover enough of the planet prior to that time. Thermometer readings around the world have been rising since the Industrial Revolution, and the causes are a blend of human activity and some natural variability with the preponderance of evidence saying humans are mostly responsible.

The global temperature record represents an average over the entire surface of the planet. The temperatures we experience locally and in short periods can fluctuate significantly due to predictable cyclical events (night and day, summer and winter) and hard-to-predict wind and precipitation patterns. But the global temperature mainly depends on how much energy the planet receives from the Sun and how much it radiates back into space—quantities that change very little. The amount of energy radiated by the Earth depends significantly on the chemical composition of the atmosphere, particularly the amount of heat-trapping greenhouse gases.

Earth's temperature has risen by 0.14° F (0.08° C) per decade since 1880, and the rate of warming over the past 40 years is more than twice that: 0.32° F (0.18° C) per decade since 1981 (Lindsey et al.).

Despite the overall increase, global temperatures have been increasing with fluctuations during the decades. The average global annual temperature hovered around 13.7 °C (56.7 °F) from the 1880s through the 1910s. During the 1920s to 1940s, temperatures climbed about 0.1 °C (0.18 °F) each decade. Mean global temperatures then stabilized at roughly 14.0°C (57.2 °F) until the 1980s. The world has mainly grown hotter since 1980, at a rate of nearly 0.2 °C (0.36 °F) per decade. The annual global temperature from 2000 to 2009 was 0.61 °C (1.1 °F) higher than the average temperature for 1951 to 1980. If the current rate of increase continues, the world will warm by 2 °C (3.6 °F) in the next century (Osborn, 2021).
